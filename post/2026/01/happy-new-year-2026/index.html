Happy New Year 2026

1 January 2026    sitenew year   Permalink

2025 was a busy and challenging year (the only kind these days it seems). On the personal front, I managed to stick to a couple of last year’s resolutions.

I have always found it difficult to keeping to a daily regime. I get absorbed in some problem, and it’s suddenly the next day. But I managed to make a short diary entry every day in 2025, thanks to a basic template in the Day One app, and I wish I’d started this years ago.

I was a voracious reader of science fiction as a teenager, but as an adult my reading dropped off in favour of other distractions. I’ve been trying to get back it for a while, but 2025 was the first year where it stuck - partly by limiting time on social media, and partly by bootstrapping the habit on a series of very readable novels.

I finished 28 books in 2025: 13 in the science fiction genre, 10 urban fantasy and 5 general fiction.

Science Fiction

Some Desperate Glory by Emily Tesh

Shortlisted for several awards, and winner of the Hugo. This space opera begins in a claustrophobic, radicalised outpost of humanity, but gathers pace when the protagonists escape to the wider universe. It has interesting things to say and compelling - not always likeable - characters.

Annihilation by Jeff VanderMeer

Book one of the Southern Reach series. I loved the film, directed by Alex Garland and starring Natalie Portman, and finally got around to reading the source novel. The atmosphere that VanderMeer conjures is deeply unsettling, and highlights how most stories of first contact with alien species assumes that we will eventually understand and be able to contextualise the experience. Instead, this first contact is baffling, unexplained, orthogonal. The film is a loose adaptation, but if anything is more dreamlike and strange than the novel.

These Burning Stars and On Vicious Worlds by Bethany Jacobs

These Burning Stars was hands-down my favourite book of the year. It is hard to believe it’s a first novel, albeit from a teacher of creative writing. It is incredibly accomplished, with compelling characters, an intricate plot, and tremendous world-building. The sequel, On Vicious Worlds, could hardly reach those heights, but was a compelling addition to the sequence. I can’t wait to read the final volume, This Brutal Moon, which has just been published.

The Ministry of Time by Kaliane Bradley

This book has an interesting SF premise, and was shortlisted for several awards and heavily feted by the mainstream press. Ultimately I think it didn’t deliver on the promise - the main protagonist is passive to a frustrating degree, and it’s hard to tell if the author is making a larger point or couldn’t give her more agency within the restrictions of the plot. The book has a measured pace for most of its length, slowly revealing details of the mysterious ministry and the larger conspiracy, but the final act feels rushed, with several infodumps which feel at odds with the care taken to that point, and which confuse rather than explain the preceding events.

Velocity Weapon by Megan E O’Keefe

Book one of the Protectorate series, this novel was shortlisted for the Philip K Disk award. I wanted to love this space opera more, but it becomes increasingly enjoyable as the plot threads come together. It features a major plot twist which I personally found frustrating, as I had become invested in the scenario that opens the book.

The Last Gifts of the Universe by Riley August

This is a short, cosy SF novel - brother and sister search space for information that may help repel a cataclysmic, off-screen threat. The focus remains firmly on our protagonists (and their cat), and I enjoyed the journey with them.

The Murderbot Diaries by Martha Wells

I had read the first book in this series, All Systems Red, several years ago, but the high price of the sequels (many of which are novellas) had delayed me from completing it. That changed when the entire sequence was sold by Humble Bundle. The Murderbot books are smart, droll, and set in a compelling universe. I’m looking forward to reading the new instalment, Platform Decay, and to see how Apple have translated the series to TV.

  • Artificial Condition (novella)
  • Rogue Protocol (novella)
  • Exit Strategy (novella)
  • Network Effect
  • Fugitive Telemetry (novella)
  • System Collapse

Urban fantasy

I read several entries in two series of hard-to-categorise novels: set in present day and featuring young protagonists dealing with mysterious occult situations. I read six of the the Rivers of London series by Ben Aaronovitch first, and later the first five books in the Laundry Files, by Charles Stross. It’s clear that the former bear a debt to the latter.

Rivers of London series by Ben Aaronovitch

This series follows a young police constable as he is seconded to a secret department of the London Metropolitan Police Force, to manage and counter the occult/fantastical citizens that live alongside us. The books are very enjoyable and have some laugh-out-loud moments. There is a little variability, but the best are are very rewarding.

  • Rivers of London
  • Moon over Soho
  • Whispers Underground
  • Broken Homes
  • Foxglove Summer
  • The Hanging Tree

Laundry Files series by Charles Stross

I have read most of Charles Stross’s SF output, including Accelerando, one of my favourite books of all time. This series is somewhat more ambitious in scope that Rivers of London, and follows a reluctant recruit to a secret government department tasked with managing outbreaks of a Lovecraftian nature. The explanation for the “magic” used to counter these threats is neat, and argues for an SF categorisation: certain computation can narrow the distance between parallel dimensions, enough that bi-directional access can be obtained.

  • The Atrocity Archives
  • The Jennifer Morgue
  • The Fuller Memorandum
  • The Apocalypse Codex

General Fiction

Orbital by Samantha Harvey

Winner of the Booker prize, this is a tone poem of a novel that follows the astronauts aboard the International Space Station over the course of a day, as they work, interact and observe events on the planet below. It features some beautiful writing, and evokes a dreamy, slightly detached, atmosphere that suggests the effects of separating people from the greater part of humanity over a long period.

Red Team Blues by Cory Doctorow

Cory is prolific across genres, and I have read his SF for many years. This book is more of a thriller, and follows forensic accountant Martin Hench through some Silicon Valley skulduggery. As always with Cory, it is deeply researched and propulsive.

Ascension by Oliver Harris

This is a spy story set on the isolated, geopolitically significant island of Ascension. I loved the writing and the rising sense of urgency as secrets are revealed.

The Secret Hours by Mick Herron

Of course I love the Apple TV series Slow Horses, but this largely stand-alone novel is the first I’ve read by its creator, Mick Herron. It feels like a book of two halves. After a short and exciting prologue, there follows lengthy coverage of an enquiry into the secret service that I found fairly tedious. It is only later in the novel, as threads are tied together, that it became more compelling. Perhaps this was the wrong book of the series to start with.

The Thursday Murder Club by Richard Osman

Impossible to avoid, I finally read the first book in this cost crime series. I enjoyed spending time with the characters, although I think it suffers from the first-book syndrome of including way too much plot.
